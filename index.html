About PLOM

Foundation models, large language models, and agentic systems are rapidly reshaping the privacy landscape of machine learning. A common feature across these paradigms is their profound and intricate opacity: for a variety of reasons (including fierce commercial competition, the scale of training data, and confidentiality constraints), very little information about these systems is publicly disclosed. Foundation models are often released without clear details on their training data and training process; large language models go through complex post-training and alignment pipelines on datasets crafted from human-AI interactions; agentic systems introduce long-horizon data use, tool-mediated information flows, and data repurposing across contexts that users and institutions may not fully anticipate. Put otherwise, as these systems are trained on partially public large-scale data, deployed through APIs and interactive interfaces, and increasingly connected to tools, external applications, and other agents, they create new pathways for privacy failures arising from memorization, retrieval, tool use, memory, and cross-context information flow. The assessment of these privacy risks is made extremely challenging by their partial transparency: researchers cannot examine the internal structure of these models, inspect the data, audit every step of the training pipeline or retrain shadow models to evaluate the effect of privacy interventions. Together, these developments challenge existing definitions, benchmarks, mitigation strategies, governance frameworks, and legal notions of consent, purpose limitation, and accountability.

This workshop will provide a timely forum for examining memorization and privacy risks across opaque models. with particular emphasis on how these risks should be defined, measured, mitigated, and governed in real-world deployment settings. It will bring together researchers from privacy, machine learning, human-computer interaction (HCI), law, and agentic AI to examine a central question: \textit{what should privacy mean when increasingly opaque models are trained, adapted, deployed, and allowed to act on increasingly sensitive information?} From a technical perspective, we need new definitions, evaluation strategies, threat models, evaluations, mitigations, and formal guarantees for systems that may memorize training data and can only be partially accessed by auditors. From a legal, policy, and deployment perspective, we must also ask: who is responsible when private information is exposed through a model output, a retrieved document, or an agent-to-agent interaction? What types of control mechanisms are available to each stakeholder within their respective scope of authority? How should privacy risks be disclosed to users, audited by organizations, and governed across the AI lifecycle? By connecting these technical and governance questions, the workshop aims to develop a shared research agenda for privacy in real-world AI deployments.
